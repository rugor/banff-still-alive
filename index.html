Historical Context —

  • Written in 1936, in the wake of post WW I Fascism
  • Hitler had already come to power
  • Benjamin had already fled the Nazi Government and relocated to Paris

What he addressed —

  • Traditional aesthetic values of 'creativity and genius, eternal value and mystery — concepts whose uncontrolled application would lead to a processing of data in the Fascist sense' (p. 218)
  • He believed these values were generated by the bourgeois capitalist class, and amounted to what he called a 'theology of art' (p. 224)
  • Introduced concepts which were useless for the purposes of Fascism, but were useful for the formulation of 'revolutionary demands in the politics of art' (p. 218)

How he addressed —

  • By creating categories outside of traditional aesthetic values rooted in the ritual
  • By considering the implications of an object's reproducability and introducing the concept of the 'aura' to highlight the evolution of art objects in the age of reproducabilty

Getting to 'the aura' —

  • The earliest works of art were originated in the service of a ritual
  • The work of art has never entirely been separated from it's ritual function
  • An example:

    Certain religious works of art can only be seen by members of a high order, or perhaps only from privileged vantage points within a specific location (p. 225)
The authenticity of a thing is the essence of all that is transmissible from its beginning, ranging from its substantive duration to its testimony to the history which it has experienced

(p. 221)

Aura —

  • An atmosphere of detached and transcendent beauty experienced while 'taking view of an object'
  • Authority is gained through the object's historical existence, encompassing changes in ownership and changes in physical condition
  • Comparative to a feeling of proximity when experiencing nature (p. 222)
  • In an art object the aura is the most sensitive nucleus, and it's authenticity vulnerable as opposed to natural objects (p. 221)
  • When the process of mechanical reproduction is introduced, the 'aura' of the work of art withers

On imitation —

  • An imitation requires reference to an external model
  • The relationship between an imitation and the original is inherently hierarchical
  • The imitation and the original cannot be united, because then the imitation would become identical to the original and cease to be an imitation

On the process of mechanical reproduction —

  • 'The technique of reproduction detaches the reproduced object from the domain of tradition'
  • A plurality of copies is substituted for a unique existence (p. 221)
  • So the 'aura' of a reproduced work exists in place of a unique work with defined specificity

...for the first time in world history, mechanical reproduction emancipates the work of art from its parasitical dependence on ritual. To an ever greater degree the work of art reproduced becomes the work of art designed for reproducibility.

(p. 224, emphasis mine)

  • Reproduction is a capitalized mode of production
  • 'Designing' a work of art to engage with capitalized production is a completely different mode of making. This amounts to a change in perception.

Organization of perception —

  • The fifth century, with its great shifts of population, saw the birth of the late Roman art industry and the Vienna Genesis, and there developed not only an art different from that of antiquity but also a new kind of perception (p. 222)
  • Riegl and Wickhoff drew conclusions concerning the organization of perception, but they did not show the social transformations expressed by these changes in perception
  • It is now possible to show the social causes of changes in the medium of contemporary perception

Social causes of these changes are examined through three mediums —

  • Photography
  • Film
  • Architecture

Photography

From a photographic negative, for example, one can make any number of prints; to ask for the "authentic" print makes no sense. But the instant the criterion of authenticity ceases to be applicable to artistic production, the total function of art is reversed. Instead of being based on ritual, it begins to be based on another practice – politics.

(p. 224)

  • Works of art are received on different planes, polarities of cult and exhibition value emerge (p. 224)
  • In photography, exhibition value begins to displace cult value all along the line. But cult value does not give way without resistance. It. retires into an ultimate retrenchment: the human. (p. 225)
  • Portrait photography offers the last refuge for the cult value of the picture (p. 226)
  • He says Atget's photographs of Paris streets are like 'scenes of a crime'

Atget's photographs —

  • Become 'standard evidence for historical occurrences' and acquire 'hidden political significance'
  • Atget himself considered his pictures merely 'documents'
  • In contrast to portraits of loved ones, Atget does not photograph people, or blurs them with long exposures
  • What is not present in a photograph can say just as much as what is present — Atget makes this clear
  • The importance of photo captioning for directing the viewer of a picture becomes important

Film

  • Film relies on the bringing together of 'optical tests' to constitute a whole (p. 228)
  • The film actor, unlike the stage actor does not have to respond to an audience
  • Instead, the actor performs a series of optical tests before the camera which is synonymous with the perspective of the audience
  • This innovation essentially puts the audience in the position of the critic; the audience's approach is one of testing — not the approach to which cult values may be exposed (p. 229)
  • 'It is inherent in the technique of the film as well as that of sports that everybody who witnesses its accomplishments is somewhat of an expert' (p. 231)
  • Because man is acting before a mechanical contrivance, he must operate with his whole living person — yet forgoing its aura (p. 229)
  • The aura is tied to the actor's presence, there can be no replica of it

Dadaism —

  • Attempted to create by pictorial — and literary — means the effects which the public today seeks in the film (p. 237)
  • Sacrificed the market values which are so characteristic of the film in favor of higher ambitions
  • Aimed to achieve a relentless destruction of the aura of their creations, which they branded as reproductions within the very means of production (p. 238)
  • Made lasting contemplation of their work problematic — their work strikes quickly and disrupts the possibility of contemplation

Film is similarly distracting and, after Duhamel, creates polarities in the viewer of distraction and contemplation

Architeture

  • 'A man who concentrates before a work of art is absorbed by it' (p. 239)
  • Similarly, architecture is received collectively in a state of distraction
  • Because architecture is so omnipresent in contemporary society, we are continually absorbed in it's presence
  • We only really 'notice' it when these habits of perception are momentarily broken
  • The 'shock' effect of film meets this mode of reception halfway (p. 240)

Conclusion

  • The masses have a right to change property relations
  • Fascism offers the proletarian class only a chance to express themselves, while maintaining existing property relations
  • War is the only way to mobilize all of today's technical resources while maintaining the traditional property system (p. 241)

The self-alienation within Fascism has become so extreme, that the destruction of humanity itself has become an aesthetic experience of the first order

Question —

  1. How does Benjamin's theory of the 'aura' apply to digital works of art, which are absent of any 'original'? Does this change Benjamin's theory?
